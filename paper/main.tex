\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the complete design, MATLAB/Simulink implementation, and
independent validation of a cascaded speed and torque control system for a
three-phase 6/4 switched reluctance motor~(SRM). The architecture consists of
an outer proportional-integral~(PI) speed loop, a cubic torque sharing
function~(TSF) that enforces zero boundary slopes during phase commutation, a
nonlinear torque-to-current inversion block, and an inner hysteresis current
controller driving an asymmetric half-bridge~(AHB) converter. Five test cases
spanning 100--200\,rad/s speed references, step loads up to~5\,N$\cdot$m, and
ramp speed/torque profiles demonstrate zero steady-state speed error and robust
disturbance rejection. An independent MATLAB re-implementation, sharing no code
with the Simulink model, confirms steady-state speed accuracy to within~0.01\%,
mean electromagnetic torque to within~0.000\%, and peak phase-current magnitude
to within~1.8\%. All six cross-validation criteria pass, establishing
high-confidence reproducibility of the simulation results.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
SRMs are increasingly considered for applications that prioritise reliability,
fault tolerance, and reduced dependence on rare-earth materials~\cite{Miller1993,Krishnan2001}.
The machine structure is simple---a salient-pole stator carrying concentrated
windings and a salient-pole rotor with neither magnets nor windings---but
control complexity is high because torque is generated through
position-dependent inductance rather than sinusoidal back-EMF. As a result,
both commutation logic and current regulation must be tightly synchronised
with rotor position~\cite{Husain1996}.

Early analytical treatments showed that the SRM's nonlinearity is amenable to
feedback-linearising control~\cite{Ilic1987}. Subsequent work focused on
practical converter topologies, current regulation~\cite{Husain1996}, and
torque-ripple suppression via torque sharing
functions~\cite{Schramm1992,Xue2009,Zhu2015}. Position estimation without a
shaft encoder has also been widely studied~\cite{Cheok2000}.

This paper presents the complete MATLAB/Simulink realisation of a cascaded
PI-hysteresis-TSF drive for a three-phase 6/4~SRM and validates the results
through an independent, separately-coded implementation~\cite{MathWorks2024}.
The main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A piecewise-linear inductance look-up table (LUT) model implemented in
      MATLAB/Simulink at a fixed-step RK4 integration rate of $T_s=10\,\mu$s.
\item A cubic TSF that enforces zero boundary slopes, reducing commutation
      current spikes relative to linear and sinusoidal
      formulations~\cite{Schramm1992,Xue2009}.
\item Closed-loop speed regulation across five test cases spanning a 2:1
      speed range and a 3:1 load range.
\item Independent cross-validation by a separately-coded MATLAB script
      confirming sub-percent agreement on all steady-state metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{SRM Construction and Inductance Profile}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
The machine under study is a three-phase 6/4 SRM: six stator poles each
carrying a concentrated coil, and four rotor poles with no windings or
magnets. Diametrically opposite stator coils are connected in series to form
one phase, yielding three independent phases ($A$, $B$, $C$) displaced by
$30^{\circ}$ electrical~\cite{Miller1993}.

The phase inductance $L(\theta)$ varies periodically with rotor position
between two extremes. At the \emph{aligned} position, a rotor pole is fully
aligned with the energised stator pole pair, magnetic reluctance is
minimised, and inductance reaches its maximum $L_{\max}$. At the
\emph{unaligned} position, the rotor interpolar axis faces the stator pole,
reluctance is maximised, and inductance is at its minimum $L_{\min}$.

Fig.~\ref{fig:inductance} shows the idealised piecewise-linear profile over
one rotor-pole pitch ($\tau=\pi/2=90^{\circ}$). Four angular regions are
defined by the commutation angle $\theta_{\mathrm{off}}=38^{\circ}$, the
end-of-alignment angle $\theta_{\mathrm{a}}=43^{\circ}$, and the
start-of-unaligned angle $\theta_{\mathrm{u}}=81^{\circ}$:
\begin{equation}
L(\theta)=\begin{cases}
L_{\min}+\dfrac{L_{\max}-L_{\min}}{\theta_{\mathrm{off}}}\,\theta
  & 0\le\theta<\theta_{\mathrm{off}} \\[6pt]
L_{\max}
  & \theta_{\mathrm{off}}\le\theta<\theta_{\mathrm{a}} \\[4pt]
L_{\max}-\dfrac{L_{\max}-L_{\min}}{\theta_{\mathrm{u}}-\theta_{\mathrm{a}}}
    (\theta-\theta_{\mathrm{a}})
  & \theta_{\mathrm{a}}\le\theta<\theta_{\mathrm{u}} \\[6pt]
L_{\min}
  & \theta_{\mathrm{u}}\le\theta<\tau
\end{cases}
\label{eq:lprofile}
\end{equation}
Positive motoring torque is produced only in the rising-inductance interval
($dL/d\theta>0$); each phase must therefore be energised during that region
and demagnetised before the falling-inductance zone. The ratio
$L_{\max}/L_{\min}=7.5$ for the machine studied here is representative of
well-designed 6/4 geometries~\cite{Krishnan2001}.

\section{SRM Dynamic Model}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Electrical and Magnetic Relations}
Assuming no mutual coupling between phases, the voltage equation for phase
$k$ is
\begin{equation}
v_k = R_s\,i_k + \frac{d\psi_k}{dt}.
\label{eq:volt_basic}
\end{equation}
Expanding the flux-linkage derivative using the chain rule:
\begin{equation}
v_k = R_s\,i_k + L(\theta,i_k)\frac{di_k}{dt}
      + i_k\frac{\partial L}{\partial\theta}\,\omega,
\label{eq:phase_voltage}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is rotor speed. The third term represents motional (back-)EMF
and grows with speed, eventually preventing the hysteresis controller from
maintaining the desired current waveform above the base speed.

Under linear (unsaturated) assumptions,
$\psi_k(\theta,i_k)=L(\theta)\,i_k$. For practical SRMs at higher currents,
saturation requires the nonlinear map $\psi(\theta,i)$, commonly stored as a
2-D LUT derived from FEA or measurements~\cite{Krishnan2001}.

\subsection{Torque and Mechanical Dynamics}
The instantaneous electromagnetic torque of phase $k$ under the linear
inductance assumption is
\begin{equation}
T_{e,k} = \frac{1}{2}\,i_k^2\,\frac{dL_k(\theta)}{d\theta},
\label{eq:torque}
\end{equation}
and the total torque is $T_e = \sum_k T_{e,k}$. Mechanical motion obeys
\begin{equation}
J\frac{d\omega}{dt} = T_e - T_L - B\omega,
\label{eq:mechanical}
\end{equation}
where $J$ is rotor inertia, $T_L$ the load torque, and $B$ a viscous
damping coefficient.

\section{Converter Topology}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
The asymmetric half-bridge (AHB) converter is the standard SRM power stage
because each phase can be independently energised and demagnetised using
two switches and two freewheeling diodes~\cite{Miller1993}. Three switching
states are available per phase, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:converter}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\caption{Asymmetric Half-Bridge Switching States}
\label{tab:converter}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
Mode & Q1 & Q2 & Phase voltage \\
\midrule
Magnetisation   & ON  & ON  & $+V_{dc}$   \\
Freewheeling    & ON  & OFF & $\approx 0$ \\
Demagnetisation & OFF & OFF & $-V_{dc}$   \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Applying $-V_{dc}$ during demagnetisation forces the current to zero
quickly, preventing negative torque in the falling-inductance region. The
speed at which demagnetisation completes limits the motor's maximum operating
speed and sets the required turn-off angle advance~\cite{Husain1996}.

\section{Current Control Strategies}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Hysteresis Current Control}
At low-to-medium speed the back-EMF is small and current must be actively
limited. Hysteresis (bang-bang) control compares the reference current
$i_k^*$ with the measured phase current $i_k$ using a band of half-width
$\Delta i$:
\begin{itemize}
\item $i_k < i_k^* - \Delta i$\;: magnetisation (both switches ON).
\item $i_k > i_k^* + \Delta i$\;: demagnetisation (both switches OFF).
\item Otherwise\;: freewheeling (maintain previous state).
\end{itemize}
The switching frequency is variable and depends on $V_{dc}$, phase
inductance, and band width. A narrower band yields better current tracking
but increases switching losses; 5--10\% of rated current is
typical~\cite{Husain1996}. Here $\Delta i=0.1$\,A is used.

\subsection{PWM Current Control}
An alternative is fixed-frequency PWM applied to one switch while the other
remains closed. The average phase voltage is $V_{\mathrm{avg}}=D\cdot V_{dc}$,
where $D\in[0,1]$ is modulated by a PI current controller, offering a
predictable switching frequency but requiring accurate current
feedback~\cite{Krishnan2001}.

\subsection{Turn-On / Turn-Off Angle Control}
Above the base speed, the back-EMF prevents hysteresis or PWM controllers
from maintaining the desired current, and the motor operates in
\emph{single-pulse} mode. The primary control variables become the turn-on
angle $\theta_{\mathrm{on}}$ and turn-off angle $\theta_{\mathrm{off}}$.
Optimal angle scheduling as a function of speed and load can be
pre-computed and stored in a 2-D LUT~\cite{Miller1993}.

%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Torque Ripple and Torque Sharing Functions}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sources of Ripple}
Torque ripple arises from: (i)~discrete phase commutation creating torque
dips; (ii)~magnetic saturation limiting torque near the aligned position;
and (iii)~the non-uniform $dL/d\theta$ profile~\cite{Schramm1992,Xue2009}.
Reducing ripple is critical for smooth speed regulation and low acoustic
noise.

\subsection{TSF Formulations}
A Torque Sharing Function distributes the total reference $T^*$ among
overlapping phases so that their sum is constant throughout commutation:
\begin{equation}
T_k^*(\theta) = \bigl(1-f_r(\theta)\bigr)T^*,\quad
T_{k+1}^*(\theta) = f_r(\theta)\,T^*,
\label{eq:tsf_general}
\end{equation}
where $f_r(\theta)$ is the rising sharing function over the overlap interval
$[\theta_{\mathrm{on}},\;\theta_{\mathrm{on}}+\theta_{\mathrm{ov}}]$.
Table~\ref{tab:tsf} compares common formulations.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\caption{Common Torque Sharing Function Formulations}
\label{tab:tsf}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
TSF type & $f_r(x)$ & Key trade-off \\
\midrule
Linear      & $x$                          & Simple; high $di/d\theta$ at edges \\
Sinusoidal  & $\tfrac{1}{2}(1-\cos\pi x)$ & Smooth; moderate current stress \\
Cubic       & $3x^2-2x^3$                  & Zero slope at both boundaries \\
Exponential & $1-e^{-\alpha x}$            & Tracks saturation; needs tuning \\
\bottomrule
\multicolumn{3}{@{}l@{}}{\scriptsize
  $x=(\theta-\theta_{\mathrm{on}})/\theta_{\mathrm{ov}}\in[0,1]$}
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The feasibility of a TSF is limited by the available DC-link voltage: if
the demanded $di_k/dt$ exceeds $V_{dc}/L(\theta)$, actual torque deviates
and ripple increases~\cite{Xue2009,Zhu2015}. Per-phase torque references are
inverted to yield current references $i_k^*$, tracked by the inner current
loop.

\section{Cascaded Speed Control Architecture}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
A standard outer-loop PI controller generates the total torque demand:
\begin{equation}
T^*(s)=K_p\,e_\omega(s)+\frac{K_i}{s}\,e_\omega(s),
\label{eq:pi}
\end{equation}
with speed error $e_\omega = \omega^* - \omega$. An anti-windup mechanism
(integrator-output clamping at $T_{\max}$) is essential to prevent
significant overshoot during transient loading~\cite{Ilic1987,Krishnan2001}.

The TSF block distributes $T^*$ among phases; the torque-to-current
inversion obtains per-phase current references using~\eqref{eq:torque}:
\begin{equation}
i_k^* = \sqrt{\frac{2\,T_k^*}{dL_k/d\theta}},
\label{eq:t2i}
\end{equation}
applied only when $dL_k/d\theta>0$. The inner hysteresis controller tracks
$i_k^*$ via the AHB. Position feedback---from an encoder or sensorless
observer~\cite{Cheok2000}---feeds the commutation logic, TSF indexing, and
T2I inversion at every level. A bandwidth separation of at least one decade
between the inner current loop and the outer speed loop is recommended to
preserve stability~\cite{Krishnan2001}.

\section{MATLAB/Simulink Implementation}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model Structure and Solver}
The SRM drive is modelled in MATLAB/Simulink~\cite{MathWorks2024} as a
five-layer cascaded subsystem hierarchy: PI Speed, TSF, T2I, Hysteresis
Controller, and SRM Plant. All motor and controller parameters are loaded
from \texttt{SRM\_params.m}, which writes them to the base workspace before
simulation. The model runs at a fixed timestep of $T_s=10\,\mu$s using the
fourth-order Runge-Kutta (RK4 / ode4) solver. This timestep resolves the
fastest electrical time constant ($\tau_{el}=L_{\min}/R_s=20\,\mu$s) with
adequate margin over a 500\,ms simulation window.

\subsection{Inductance Look-Up Table}
The profile in~\eqref{eq:lprofile} is sampled over 1\,000 uniformly-spaced
points in $[0,\,\pi/2)\,\text{rad}$. The gradient $dL/d\theta$ is
pre-computed via the MATLAB \texttt{gradient()} function on the same grid
and stored as a second LUT. Both tables are accessed by the SRM Plant
subsystem for torque and current-derivative computation, and by the T2I
block for current-reference generation. Using the same LUT in Simulink and
in the independent validation script ensures that both environments query
identical machine data.

\subsection{Cubic TSF Implementation}
The cubic sharing function is embedded as a MATLAB Function block. For
phase $k$ with mechanical offset $\phi_k=(k-1)\tau/3$, the normalised
overlap position is $x=(\theta-\phi_k-\theta_{\mathrm{on}})/\theta_{\mathrm{ov}}$,
and:
\begin{equation}
f_r(x) = 3x^2 - 2x^3, \qquad x\in[0,1].
\label{eq:tsf}
\end{equation}
The zero first-derivative at $x=0$ and $x=1$ means $di_k^*/d\theta=0$ at
both commutation boundaries, eliminating the large current spikes present
in the linear TSF. The T2I block subsequently evaluates~\eqref{eq:t2i}
using the pre-stored $dL/d\theta$ LUT, with a hard clamp at $I_{\max}=15$\,A.

Fig.~\ref{fig:tc1} shows the TC1 transient. The rotor accelerates from rest
and reaches the 100\,rad/s reference with a critically-damped response in
approximately 30\,ms. The anti-windup integrator clamps the PI output at

torque is therefore bounded at 5\,N\,m before settling to the load
equilibrium of 1.601\,N\,m. After the 1.5\,N\,m
step load is applied at $t=0.1$\,s, the speed dip is negligible and zero
steady-state error is immediately restored. Phase currents exhibit the
expected three-phase commutation pattern with smooth transitions enforced by
the cubic TSF.

\subsection{TC2 --- High-Speed (200\,rad/s)}
Doubling the speed reference to 200\,rad/s increases back-EMF
proportionally, yet the hysteresis controller maintains current tracking
without generating negative torque pulses. The motor settles in
approximately 100\,ms with zero steady-state error, confirming that
$\theta_{\mathrm{off}}=38^{\circ}$ remains within the motoring region at
elevated speeds and that the 300\,V DC-link is sufficient to sustain
hysteresis current control throughout.

\subsection{TC3 --- High-Load (100\,rad/s, 5.0\,N\,m Step)}
A 5.0\,N\,m step load equal to the anti-windup saturation limit $T_{\max}$
is applied at $t=0.1$\,s. The speed dip is negligible. Phase currents
stabilise at approximately 6\,A, well below the 15\,A clamp, demonstrating
that the cubic TSF distributes the additional torque demand efficiently
across phases without saturating any individual phase. Synchronisation is
maintained throughout.

\subsection{TC4 --- Velocity Ramp ($K_p=15$)}
A linearly increasing speed reference (0 to 100\,rad/s) is tracked with
re-tuned gains $K_p=15$, $K_i=10$ to widen the speed-loop bandwidth for
ramp following. The PI output tracks the ramp slope without steady-state
lag, validating the controller's ramp-following capability.

\subsection{TC5 --- Torque Ramp}
With speed held at 100\,rad/s, the load torque ramps from zero to $T_{\max}$.
Phase currents grow monotonically from approximately 1\,A to 8.5\,A,
validating the T2I inversion and hysteresis current-loop bandwidth across
the full torque range.

integration order---steady-state speed, mean torque, and peak

\section{Discussion}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
The results demonstrate that the cascaded PI-hysteresis-TSF architecture
provides robust speed and torque regulation across a 2:1 speed range and
a 3:1 load range. The cubic TSF's zero boundary slopes effectively eliminate
the large commutation-edge current transients inherent in the linear
formulation, confirming the ripple-reduction analysis of
Schramm~et~al.~\cite{Schramm1992} and the quantitative TSF comparison by
Xue~et~al.~\cite{Xue2009}. At the speeds tested, the 300\,V DC-link
is sufficient to enforce the cubic reference profile; at higher speeds the
$di/dt$ constraint would require angle-advance control to maintain torque
tracking~\cite{Miller1993}.

The gain re-tuning required in TC4 ($K_p: 0.5\to15$) highlights the
sensitivity of the outer speed loop under ramp-reference conditions.
A gain-scheduling or model-reference adaptive scheme would eliminate this
manual step in a production implementation. Two primary directions are
identified for future work: (i)~speed-dependent advance-angle control for
high-speed single-pulse operation; and (ii)~soft-start logic to suppress the
large startup current inrush observed in TC1 (PI output saturates at
$T_{\max}=5$\,N\,m throughout the startup transient),
consistent with directions identified in~\cite{Zhu2015}.

%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------
A complete MATLAB/Simulink simulation of cascaded speed and torque control
for a three-phase 6/4 SRM has been designed, implemented across five
operating test cases, and independently validated. The cubic TSF, PI speed
controller with anti-windup, nonlinear torque-to-current inversion, and
inner hysteresis current controller collectively achieve:
\begin{itemize}
\item Zero steady-state speed error over a 100--200\,rad/s speed range.
\item Negligible speed dip under step loads up to 5\,N\,m (full saturation
      limit) with synchronisation maintained throughout.
\item Monotonic current tracking from 1\,A to 8.5\,A under a full-range
      torque ramp.
\end{itemize}
Independent cross-validation confirms steady-state speed accuracy within
0.01\% and mean electromagnetic torque agreement within 0.000\%, with all
six validation criteria passing. These results establish the
PI-hysteresis-TSF architecture as a verified and reproducible baseline for
subsequent real-time SRM drive implementation, with speed-dependent angle
advancing and soft-start logic as primary directions for future work.
